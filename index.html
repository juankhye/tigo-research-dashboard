First Principles

LatAm Telco Primer

New to the space? Here's everything you need to understand Millicom's business from the ground up.

What is ARPU and why does it matter?
ARPU (Average Revenue Per User) measures the average monthly revenue generated per subscriber. In LatAm, mobile ARPU typically ranges from $3-8/month for prepaid users versus $15-30/month for postpaid. Higher ARPU means better monetization of each customer. Millicom's strategy of migrating prepaid users to postpaid directly increases ARPU without needing new customers. Fixed-broadband ARPU is typically $20-40/month, and convergent bundles (mobile + broadband + TV) can reach $50+, which is why Millicom pushes fixed-mobile convergence.
Why do EBITDA margins matter so much for telcos?
Telecom is capital-intensive: you must build towers, lay fiber, buy spectrum licenses, and maintain networks before earning a dollar. EBITDA (Earnings Before Interest, Taxes, Depreciation, Amortization) strips out these large non-cash charges to show operating cash generation. A 45% EBITDA margin means $0.45 of every revenue dollar becomes operating cash. This cash funds CapEx, debt service, and dividends. Millicom's margin expansion from 37% to 48%+ is transformational because every 1 percentage point of margin on $8.4B revenue equals ~$84M of additional cash generation annually.
Understanding leverage in telecom
Leverage (Net Debt / EBITDA) measures how many years of operating cash it would take to repay all debt. For telcos, 2-3x is considered normal because the business generates stable, recurring cash flows that support debt servicing. Below 2x is conservative; above 3x raises concerns. Millicom's leverage peaks at 2.5x in 2026E due to M&A spending, then declines to 1.8x by 2028E as EBITDA grows faster than debt. This deleveraging trajectory is a key part of the investment thesis, as lower leverage reduces interest costs and increases financial flexibility for dividends and buybacks.
FX impact on LatAm telcos
Millicom earns revenue in 10+ local currencies (Guatemalan quetzal, Colombian peso, Bolivian boliviano, etc.) but reports in USD and carries USD-denominated debt. When local currencies weaken against the dollar, reported revenue shrinks even if local business is growing. Debt burden also increases in local currency terms. Bolivia is the extreme case: the official exchange rate is BOB 6.91/USD, but the practical rate is BOB 11.59/USD — a 68% devaluation that severely impacts dollar-denominated cash flows. Ecuador's dollarized economy provides a natural hedge, which was part of the strategic rationale for that acquisition.
Tower economics: own vs. lease
Cell towers are the physical infrastructure that transmit wireless signals. Historically, telcos owned their towers, but the industry has shifted toward a sale-and-leaseback model. By selling 7,000+ towers to SBA Communications for $975M, Millicom converted a depreciating asset into immediate cash while signing long-term lease agreements. This reduces CapEx (no more tower maintenance) but adds an annual lease expense. The trade-off is worthwhile when the cash can be deployed at higher returns — in Millicom's case, funding M&A that generates 15%+ returns on invested capital. The tower-light model also makes the company more nimble and asset-efficient.
Mobile vs. fixed broadband economics
Mobile has higher margins (~50-60% at the EBITDA level in strong markets) because the network serves millions of users wirelessly with relatively low per-user cost. Fixed broadband requires physically connecting each home with cable or fiber, making it more capital-intensive per subscriber. However, fixed broadband has higher ARPU and much lower churn (customers rarely switch broadband providers). The convergence strategy — bundling mobile + broadband + TV — is powerful because it increases total ARPU while dramatically reducing churn, as canceling any one service means losing the bundle discount on all services.
What is FCF yield and why do investors care?
Free Cash Flow (FCF) Yield is FCF divided by market capitalization. It represents the percentage return shareholders would receive if all free cash were distributed. An 8-11% FCF yield (Millicom's 2026-2028E range) is attractive because it means the company generates 8-11 cents of distributable cash per dollar of market value annually. This is significantly higher than bond yields or most equity alternatives. FCF yield matters more than earnings for telcos because large depreciation charges make reported earnings misleadingly low relative to actual cash generation. Millicom's growing FCF supports its new dividend policy ($3-4/share annually) while still funding debt reduction.
LatAm telco competitive dynamics
LatAm telecom markets are typically oligopolies with 2-4 major players per country. Fewer players generally means more rational pricing and higher margins — Guatemala's 2-player market produces 56% margins, while Colombia's former 4-player market historically delivered sub-40%. Telefonica's exit from multiple LatAm markets in 2024-2025 created a once-in-a-generation consolidation opportunity. Millicom seized this, acquiring Telefonica's assets in Ecuador, Uruguay, Colombia, and Chile. In Colombia, the market is consolidating from 4 to 3 players (especially after WOM's bankruptcy), which should improve pricing discipline industry-wide. America Movil (Carlos Slim's company) remains the largest competitor across the region, but Millicom's operational efficiency gives it a margin advantage in several markets.

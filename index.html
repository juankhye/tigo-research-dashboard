Executive Summary

The LatAm Telecom Consolidator, Inflecting on Cash

Millicom owns the physical networks — cell towers, fiber and coax — that carry mobile, home-internet and business connectivity to ~57m mobile customers across Latin America. The 2025–26 story is a wave of cheap M&A (capturing Telefónica's regional exit) plus a hard cost-cutting playbook, together inflecting equity free cash flow toward a double-digit yield. The Investment Thesis in Six Lines

  • Cash-flow inflection. EFCF went from ~$1m in a Q1 two years ago to $225m in Q1-26 (record) and $916m FY25A; guidance ≥$900m FY26E, UBS/HSBC see ~$1.2bn by 2027.
  • Cheap M&A roll-up. Bought Telefónica's exit — Colombia (Coltel), Ecuador (~2.4x), Uruguay, Chile JV — at low multiples; combined Colombia <2.0x EBITDA pre-synergies.
  • Colombia 4→3 consolidation. The Tigo-Movistar merger can lift margins up to 7pp; HSBC sees Colombia EBITDA $1.0bn→$1.6bn by 2030.
  • Proven cost playbook ("Project Everest"). ~27% headcount cuts drove a record ~48–49% organic margin before synergies.
  • Niel/Atlas control. Xavier Niel's ~40–44% stake drives the discipline — and is also the key governance risk.
  • The catch: after a ~50% rally to ~6.6x (peers ~5x), UBS & DNB say the upside is now largely priced in.

A Latin-American Telecom in 5 Minutes

Before any numbers, here is the mental model. Read this and the rest of the dashboard will click into place. (Every italicised term is defined in the Glossary.)

 What a telco actually is

A telecom company owns expensive physical infrastructure — cell towers, radio spectrum licensed from governments, and fiber/coax cables into homes — and rents access to it. Customers pay a recurring monthly fee. That makes revenue sticky and predictable, but the business is capital-intensive (you must keep spending capex on the network) and often debt-heavy.

 How Tigo earns

Four products: Mobile (~61% of service revenue — prepaid SIMs vs higher-value postpaid contracts), Home (broadband + TV over its own cables), B2B (connectivity/cloud/cyber for companies & governments), and Tigo Money (a mobile wallet). The profit engine is moving cheap prepaid users onto postpaid — postpaid ARPU is ~50% higher.

 The 3 numbers that matter

(1) Adjusted EBITDA & its margin — core operating profit. (2) Equity Free Cash Flow (EFCF) — the cash left for shareholders after capex, interest, leases & tax; Tigo's headline metric. (3) Net debt / EBITDA ("leverage") — balance-sheet health. The whole bull case is EFCF rising while leverage falls.

Why this is confusing right now Tigo bought Colombia's Coltel (Movistar Colombia) in early 2026 and switched it "on" in its accounts mid-quarter. That makes every reported Q1-26 number jump (revenue +45%!) even though the organic business grew only ~5%. Throughout this dashboard you'll see "reported" vs "ex-Coltel / organic" — the organic figures show the real underlying business; the reported ones show the bigger, more-levered company you actually own.
Sources: Millicom Q1-2026 earnings presentation & release (12-May-2026); AlphaSense company primer (4-Apr-2026).

The Core Thesis

The Equity Free-Cash-Flow Inflection

This is what the UBS and Morgan Stanley notes orbit: Tigo turning from a barely-cash-generative, over-levered telco into a double-digit-FCF-yield compounder. "A Q1 two years ago was just +$1m," the CFO noted — Q1-26 was +$225m. Four drivers power it.

1

The cost playbook

"Project Everest": zero-based cost reset, ~27% headcount cuts, 21% lower programming & 18% lower IT costs → record ~48–49% organic margin before any M&A synergies.

2

Accretive cheap M&A

Telefónica's exit let Tigo buy Colombia, Ecuador, Uruguay & Chile at low multiples (combined Colombia <2.0x EBITDA). Coltel adds ~$2.2bn revenue → ~$330m potential EFCF.

3

Capital discipline

Capex/sales cut from 17% (2022) toward ~13% ("capacity-driven"), plus an asset-light tower sale (>7,000 towers, ~$975m) that funds M&A and cuts leverage.

4

Deleverage + return

Leverage 2.76x → ~2.5x (YE26) → ~1.2x (2030E), with a maintained $3.00 dividend, a $2.50 special, and buyback optionality.

EFCF trajectory ($m): the inflection

The debate

UBS calls the ≥$900m guidance conservative (sees up to $1.1bn) — but in May-2026 downgraded to Neutral, arguing the upside is now priced in: consensus (~$1bn) already sits above guidance, and the stock trades ~6.6x vs peers ~5x after a ~50% rally.

The swing factor is Colombia/Coltel: whether the consolidated margin reaches the guided ~42% (UBS models 37–40%) and whether organic growth accelerates to the guided ~8% (Coltel grew only ~2% in Q1-26).

Sources: UBS (1-Apr & 24-May-2026); HSBC (5-Mar-2026); Q1-2026 earnings call.
What to Watch

Catalyst Calendar & Debt Wall

The next big event is Q2-26 earnings on 6-Aug-2026 — where management is expected to update guidance after Coltel integration (HSBC titled its note "FCFE guidance upgrade looks likely"). The capital-allocation trigger is leverage falling below 2.5x, targeted by year-end 2026.

The trigger to watch Capital returns are leverage-gated: the regular $3.00 dividend (~$507m/yr) is held flat until net debt/EBITDA drops below 2.5x (targeted YE26). Once it does, excess cash flips to buybacks and/or special dividends — HSBC models the regular dividend potentially jumping ~75% to $6.20 in 2027; DNB sees up to a ~22% remuneration yield at the 2.5x target.

The Colombia prize Colombia is now Tigo's largest revenue market. By buying EPM's half of Tigo-UNE (~$571m, 2.18x) and Telefónica's Coltel/Movistar, Tigo turns a destructive 4-player mobile market into a rational 3-player one (the #4, WOM, is bankrupt). Spotlight: The Chile JV — "Acquiring an Option"

On 10-Feb-2026 Tigo took a 49% operating stake in Telefónica Chile via a JV with NJJ (Xavier Niel's personal vehicle, 51%). Crucially the asset is not consolidated and its debt has no recourse to Tigo — the parent balance sheet is ring-fenced. Tigo paid a tiny ~$50m upfront (+ up to $150m earnout); Telefónica even injects ~$92m of cash at closing, underscoring how distressed the asset is.

Portfolio strategy: buys paused, sells active

Management's message is "integration over expansion." No new acquisitions are guided for now (Q2-25 call) — but divestitures are live, and a Niel corporate action is the wildcard.

 Buying — paused / optional

Peru & Venezuela remain on the "menu" (CEO, Q1-26 call); Mexico, Brazil & Argentina structurally excluded. Integration absorbs management bandwidth through 2026. DNB cautions a new deal would strain the balance sheet mid-integration.

 Selling — active

Tigo Money pivoted to monetization — Paraguay MFS sold ($10m + up to $7m earn-out; held-for-sale Mar-26, pending regulators) and the unit restructured to attract growth capital. Tower monetization ~complete (SBA $975m, Atis, KKR/Towernex); only ~2,000 Bolivia towers remain.

 The wildcard

A Niel / Atlas corporate action (e.g. a take-private). Atlas has crept from the 40.4% tender close (2024) to ~44–46%, with 4 of 8 board seats incl. the Chair. UBS flags a potential tender offer but does not model it.

Tigo's edge rests on rational market structures, owned converged infrastructure, brand/content, and an execution playbook — strongest in mobile/oligopoly markets, thinnest in fixed/home and B2B.

Outsized dependence on Guatemala; a new entrant there would materially dent the financial profile.

Who's in Charge & What They Do With the Cash

Control & Capital Allocation

You cannot understand Tigo without understanding Xavier Niel. The French billionaire founder of Iliad controls ~40–44% via Atlas Investissement — he drove the cost overhaul and the M&A, and is also the central governance question.

The Niel / Atlas factor

Niel's 2024 stake-build triggered a management overhaul: company-veteran Marcelo Benítez as CEO, Bart Vanhaeren as CFO, and the launch of "Project Everest." Atlas is credited with the cost discipline and decisive capital allocation.

But concentration is a risk. Kepler flagged an opportunistic ~$24/share Atlas bid (Jul-2024) it advised rejecting; the Chile JV is a related-party deal with Niel's separate NJJ vehicle. UBS flags optionality from a potential Niel tender offer (not modeled).

The unresolved question (per an ex-director): as Tigo throws off ~$500m/yr of incremental cash, will it distribute, deleverage, or double-down on more LatAm M&A?
